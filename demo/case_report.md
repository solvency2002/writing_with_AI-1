# Abstract

**Introduction.** Spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD) is an under-recognized cause of acute coronary syndrome in young women, particularly in the peripartum period.

**Patient information.** A 34-year-old woman presented 12 days postpartum with sudden anterior chest pain that had initially been attributed to musculoskeletal strain from breastfeeding.

**Clinical findings and diagnosis.** Repeat electrocardiography showed evolving anterior T-wave inversion and high-sensitivity troponin was elevated. Coronary angiography demonstrated a type 2 SCAD in the mid left anterior descending artery.

**Interventions and outcomes.** A conservative strategy with dual antiplatelet therapy, beta-blockade, and ambulatory cardiac monitoring was chosen. Left ventricular function recovered to a normal range at three months, and the patient resumed full activity at six months with structured cardiac rehabilitation.

**Conclusion.** Postpartum chest pain in a previously healthy woman warrants a low threshold for ischemic workup. SCAD should be considered before attributing symptoms to non-cardiac causes.

# Introduction

Acute coronary syndrome in young women without classical risk factors is uncommon and frequently misclassified at first contact [@hayes2018scad]. Spontaneous coronary artery dissection accounts for a disproportionate share of these events in the peripartum window and remains a teaching point that recurs across emergency medicine, obstetrics, and cardiology services [@hayes2018scad]. We describe a case in which the diagnosis was delayed by approximately 36 hours because the initial presentation was framed as musculoskeletal, and we use the case to highlight one diagnostic anchor (peripartum status) that should prompt early ischemic evaluation.

# Patient information

- **De-identified demographics.** A woman in her mid-30s, gravida 2 para 2, with an uncomplicated vaginal delivery 12 days before presentation. No prior cardiovascular history.
- **Primary concerns and symptoms.** Sudden retrosternal pressure radiating to the left arm, lasting approximately 40 minutes, accompanied by diaphoresis. The patient had been carrying her newborn at symptom onset.
- **Medical history.** No hypertension, dyslipidemia, or diabetes. Non-smoker. No prior pregnancies complicated by preeclampsia. No personal or family history of fibromuscular dysplasia or connective tissue disease.
- **Family history.** Father with hypertension diagnosed in his 60s; no premature coronary disease in first-degree relatives.
- **Psychosocial history.** Working as a registered nurse, currently on maternity leave. Lives with partner and two children. Sleep restricted to 3–4 hour intervals around feeding.
- **Past interventions.** Acetaminophen prescribed at the initial urgent-care visit for presumed costochondritis; symptoms recurred within six hours.

# Clinical findings

On re-presentation to the emergency department:

- Blood pressure 138/86 mmHg, heart rate 102/min, respiratory rate 18/min, SpO₂ 98 % on room air, temperature 36.8 °C.
- Lung fields clear; cardiac auscultation without murmurs or gallops.
- No chest-wall tenderness on repeat examination, in contrast to the initial encounter.
- No focal neurological deficit; calves non-tender with no peripheral edema.

# Diagnostic assessment

- **Diagnostic methods.** Serial 12-lead ECG, high-sensitivity troponin I at 0 and 3 hours, transthoracic echocardiography, and invasive coronary angiography. Screening for fibromuscular dysplasia included renal and cerebrovascular imaging during admission.
- **Diagnostic challenges.** The initial presentation was attributed to a musculoskeletal mechanism because chest-wall palpation reproduced discomfort and the first ECG appeared unremarkable. Postpartum status and the absence of traditional risk factors created a competing narrative that delayed ischemic workup.
- **Differential diagnoses considered.** Acute coronary syndrome due to atherothrombosis, pulmonary embolism, peripartum cardiomyopathy, aortic dissection, costochondritis, and gastroesophageal reflux.
- **Final diagnosis.** Spontaneous coronary artery dissection, Saw type 2, mid left anterior descending artery, with non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction.
- **Prognostic considerations.** Most patients managed conservatively show angiographic healing within 30 days; the risk of recurrent SCAD over five years is reported to be approximately 10–20 % and informs counseling about future pregnancy and exertion [@hayes2018scad].

# Therapeutic intervention

- **Strategy.** Conservative management without percutaneous intervention, given preserved distal flow and absence of ongoing ischemia.
- **Pharmacologic therapy.**
  - Aspirin 100 mg daily.
  - Clopidogrel 75 mg daily for 12 months.
  - Bisoprolol 2.5 mg daily, titrated to resting heart rate < 70/min.
  - Ramipril 2.5 mg daily.
- **Adjunctive measures.** Lactation consultation regarding medication compatibility; structured cardiac rehabilitation starting at four weeks; mental-health screening at first follow-up.
- **Changes in therapy.** Ramipril was discontinued at month 3 after blood pressure normalized; antiplatelet regimen reassessed at month 12 in line with shared decision-making about bleeding risk.

# Follow-up and outcomes

- **Clinician-assessed outcomes.** LVEF improved from 48 % at admission to 58 % at three months. No recurrent ischemic events through six months.
- **Patient-assessed outcomes.** Return to baseline activity tolerance by month 4; Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale within normal range at months 1 and 3.
- **Follow-up tests.** Repeat echocardiogram (month 3), cardiac MRI (month 4) showing a small apical scar without active inflammation, ambulatory blood-pressure monitoring (month 5).
- **Adherence and tolerability.** Self-reported full adherence; pill counts at month 1 and month 3 visits consistent. No bleeding events.
- **Adverse and unanticipated events.** Transient bruising on bisoprolol initiation; no hospitalizations after discharge.

# Discussion

This case illustrates two recurring lessons. First, when postpartum women present with acute chest pain, the prior probability of ischemic disease is higher than baseline for age and sex, and a normal initial ECG does not exclude SCAD [@hayes2018scad]. Second, the diagnostic narrative is sticky: once a musculoskeletal label is attached at first contact, the threshold for reopening the differential rises. Re-examining the timeline and explicitly listing competing diagnoses at the second visit was the step that changed management.

**Strengths.** The case benefited from prompt invasive imaging once ischemia was confirmed and from a multidisciplinary follow-up plan that included cardiac rehabilitation and mental-health screening.

**Limitations.** This is a single-patient observation; we cannot infer causal relationships between postpartum physiology and the dissection, and the conservative strategy reflects local practice rather than randomized evidence. Reporting guidelines for individual cases mitigate but do not eliminate selection and recall biases [@gagnier2014care].

**Take-away.** In a postpartum woman with new chest pain, treat SCAD as a default differential rather than a diagnosis of exclusion; repeat the ECG, measure high-sensitivity troponin, and document the reasoning that supports any non-cardiac label before discharge.
