Dear Editor,

Tanaka and colleagues address an important and unsettled question. Their single-center cohort of adults with sepsis adds welcome real-world data on the timing of corticosteroid therapy. The clear reporting of in-hospital mortality by treatment timing is a useful contribution to a field with few comparative studies.

The central conclusion may outrun the study design. The authors conclude that "early corticosteroids reduce mortality in sepsis and should be initiated within 6 hours of recognition." This is a retrospective cohort, and the analysis does not address confounding by indication. Clinicians choose early steroids for reasons tied to a patient's prognosis. The treated and untreated groups therefore likely differ in ways that age, sex, and comorbidity index do not capture [@pmid26978209]. An association of this design cannot support a causal claim or a timing recommendation. We suggest the finding be framed as hypothesis-generating.

The septic shock subgroup claim rests on a fragile result. The abstract states that "patients with septic shock particularly benefited," yet the subgroup comparison yielded p = 0.21. Promoting a subgroup with a p value of 0.21 to a headline benefit is a recognized form of spin in non-randomized reports [@pmid25433444]. We suggest the subgroup result be presented as exploratory, with the p value stated in the abstract.

A prospective design with a prespecified analysis would let this promising signal be tested fairly.

Sincerely,
