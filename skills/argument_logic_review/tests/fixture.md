Dear Editor,

Nguyen and colleagues address a timely question for nursing education: whether a
gamified mobile application can improve electrocardiogram (ECG) interpretation.
Their prospective nonrandomized study is valuable because it embeds the app
within an authentic clinical-skills rotation. It also uses blinded examiners and
reports both interpretation accuracy and student satisfaction. This design gives
educators a useful early view of how students learn ECG reading inside a real
rotation.

We suggest that future studies also assess retention with a paper ECG set for a
later rotation. This assessment could follow practice on the gamified app. This
study found little change in interpretation accuracy after students used the
app. It also found no difference in the subscores for rhythm recognition and
axis determination. These findings do not necessarily mean that the app lacks
educational value. The post-app test was a re-attempt of the same ECG set after
students practiced on the app and reviewed feedback as needed. If scores do not
improve on that re-attempt, retention might still appear on a later set. If the
re-attempt scores improve, the gain may reflect familiarity with the same
tracings supplied during practice. It should therefore be distinguished from a
durable improvement in students' own reading skill. Educational measurement
research separates improvement on a re-tested item from retention on later
independent items (Roediger and Karpicke, 2006).

Future studies could therefore ask students to interpret a fresh ECG set in a
later rotation. This task could follow app practice in an earlier rotation. One
design would collect a baseline paper test and randomize students to practice
with the gamified app or with usual materials. It would then assess a later
paper test using new tracings. This approach would test students' reading skill
rather than familiarity with a re-tested set. Although this study did not find a
clear improvement, a retention task might reveal an educational effect on a
later set.

The study is an important first step. Its findings do not rule out educational
value from gamified ECG apps. A fresh ECG set in a later rotation would help
test whether app practice transfers to students' own reading skill.

Sincerely,
The Authors
