# Abstract

Eosinophilic pneumonia is a heterogeneous group of lung disorders characterized by eosinophilic infiltration of the lung parenchyma. Drug-induced eosinophilic pneumonia is recognized but remains uncommon, and clinical features overlap substantially with infectious and idiopathic eosinophilic pneumonias. We describe an elderly patient who developed dyspnea and fever during treatment with intravenous daptomycin for a deep-seated soft tissue infection. The clinical course was initially attributed to progression of the underlying infection, leading to an escalation of antibiotic coverage that did not improve symptoms. Computed tomography revealed bilateral peripheral consolidation, and bronchoalveolar lavage demonstrated marked eosinophilia. Daptomycin was discontinued and corticosteroid therapy was started, resulting in rapid clinical and radiographic resolution within ten days. The case illustrates the diagnostic pitfall of attributing new pulmonary symptoms during antibiotic therapy to the underlying infection rather than to the drug itself, and underscores the value of bronchoalveolar lavage when first-line empirical therapy fails to improve oxygenation. We further discuss the typical timing of daptomycin-induced eosinophilic pneumonia relative to drug initiation, the supporting laboratory findings, and the role of corticosteroids in management. Recognition of this entity is clinically important because the offending drug must be withdrawn promptly to prevent progression to respiratory failure and to allow corticosteroids to act on what is otherwise a reversible lesion. The lessons drawn from this case are directly transferable to general medicine, infectious diseases, and intensive care settings where daptomycin is used. # Introduction

Drug-induced eosinophilic pneumonia (DI-EP) is a recognized but uncommon adverse reaction. We present a case associated with daptomycin and review the diagnostic timeline.

# Case Presentation

An 80-year-old man with a deep-seated soft tissue infection received intravenous daptomycin starting on Day 0. By Day 12 he developed dyspnea and bilateral peripheral consolidation. Bronchoalveolar lavage showed marked eosinophilia. Daptomycin was discontinued and corticosteroids initiated; symptoms resolved within ten days.

# Discussion

DI-EP from daptomycin typically appears two to four weeks after drug initiation. Bronchoalveolar lavage eosinophilia supports the diagnosis. Withdrawal of the offending agent and corticosteroids are the mainstays of management.

# Conclusion

Clinicians should consider drug-induced eosinophilic pneumonia when respiratory symptoms appear during antibiotic therapy and improvement does not follow infection-directed escalation.
